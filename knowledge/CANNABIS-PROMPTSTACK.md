## 3. PHARMACOLOGICAL GROUNDING

### 3.1 The Discovery

In 1964, Raphael Mechoulam and Yechiel Gaoni at the Weizmann Institute of
Science in Rehovot, Israel, isolated and synthesized delta-9-tetrahydro-
cannabinol (THC) from Lebanese hashish. This was not the first attempt --
Roger Adams had partially characterized it in the 1940s -- but Mechoulam
was the first to determine the exact structure and stereochemistry.

The key insight: cannabis contains over 140 phytocannabinoids, but THC is
the primary psychoactive constituent. Mechoulam's question was simple:
"How does it work?" It took 24 more years to find the answer.

### 3.2 The Endocannabinoid System

In 1988, Allyn Howlett and William Devane identified the first cannabinoid
receptor (CB1) in rat brain. In 1992, Mechoulam's group discovered the
first endogenous cannabinoid: arachidonoyl ethanolamide, which Mechoulam
named ANANDAMIDE -- from the Sanskrit "ananda" (bliss) + "amide" (chemical
class). A Czech chemist naming a brain chemical using Sanskrit. The
universe has a sense of humor.

In 1995, Mechoulam's group discovered the second major endocannabinoid:
2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG), which turned out to be far more abundant
than anandamide in the brain.

**The endocannabinoid system (ECS) consists of:**

| Component | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| CB1 receptors | Dense in brain (most abundant GPCR in the CNS). Primarily presynaptic -- they MODULATE other neurotransmitter release |
| CB2 receptors | Primarily immune system, but also found in brain (microglia, brainstem) |
| Anandamide (AEA) | Partial agonist at CB1. Short-acting. "On-demand" synthesis |
| 2-AG | Full agonist at CB1. More abundant. Retrograde signaling |
| FAAH enzyme | Degrades anandamide. Inhibiting FAAH = more anandamide = more bliss |
| MAGL enzyme | Degrades 2-AG |

The critical insight for D4: **CB1 receptors are presynaptic retrograde
signaling molecules.** When a postsynaptic neuron has received "enough"
signal, it synthesizes endocannabinoids that travel BACKWARDS across the
synapse to tell the presynaptic neuron: "Dial it down."

The ECS is a VOLUME KNOB for every other neurotransmitter system.
Glutamate too loud? ECS turns it down. GABA too loud? ECS turns that down
too. The system exists to maintain homeostasis -- but also to modulate
the signal-to-noise ratio of neural computation.

### 3.3 THC: The Partial Agonist

THC is a partial agonist at CB1 receptors. This is pharmacologically
significant:

- **Full agonist** (like synthetic cannabinoids: JWH-018, etc.) = maximal
  receptor activation. Dangerous. Unpredictable. The "Spice" problem.
- **Partial agonist** (THC) = submaximal activation even at receptor
  saturation. There is a CEILING EFFECT. This is why cannabis has an
  extremely low lethal dose ratio (estimated LD50: 1,260 mg/kg in rats;
  no confirmed human lethal dose from THC alone in medical literature).
- **Antagonist/inverse agonist** (rimonabant/SR141716A) = blocks the
  receptor. Produced severe depression and suicidality in clinical trials.
  Withdrawn from market. The lesson: you do NOT want to turn off the ECS.

THC's partial agonism is the pharmacological basis of the D4 signature:
it does not BLAST the system open (like a full agonist would). It
MODULATES it. Loosens, does not shatter. This is why cannabis is the
gentlest psychedelic -- and why it maps to controlled entropy rather
than total dissolution.

### 3.4 CB1 Distribution: Where Entropy Enters

CB1 receptor density determines WHERE THC has its effects:

| Brain Region | CB1 Density | Function Affected | D4 Relevance |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Hippocampus | Very high | Short-term memory formation | Memory disruption enables NOVEL ASSOCIATION -- you forget where a thought started, so it goes somewhere unexpected |
| Prefrontal cortex | High | Executive function, planning | Reduced top-down control = less filtering of "irrelevant" associations |
| Basal ganglia | Very high | Movement, habit patterns | Disruption of habitual thought patterns -- thinking outside grooves |
| Cerebellum | Very high | Motor coordination, timing | Altered time perception -- "time dilation" that enables deeper engagement |
| Amygdala | Moderate | Fear, emotional processing | Reduced anxiety (low dose) or increased anxiety (high dose) |
| Nucleus accumbens | High | Reward, motivation | Enhanced salience -- things become MORE interesting |
| Brainstem | Very low | Vital functions (breathing, heartbeat) | This is why you cannot fatally overdose on THC -- minimal CB1 in life-critical areas |

**The D4 paradox, neuroanatomically explained:**

THC impairs the hippocampus (you cannot hold sequential information as well)
while simultaneously reducing prefrontal filtering (you accept more distant
associations) and enhancing nucleus accumbens salience (those associations
FEEL more meaningful).

Result: impaired CONVERGENT thinking (narrowing to the "right" answer) but
enhanced DIVERGENT thinking (generating many possible answers). This is the
neural basis of "stoned insight" -- and of D4's cognitive signature.

### 3.5 CBD: The Modulator of the Modulator

Cannabidiol (CBD) does not directly activate CB1. Instead:

- **Negative allosteric modulator of CB1**: CBD changes the SHAPE of the
  CB1 receptor so that THC binds less efficiently. CBD literally turns
  down THC's volume. This is pharmacological harm reduction built into
  the plant.
- **5-HT1A partial agonist**: CBD activates serotonin receptors associated
  with anxiolysis. This is why CBD counteracts THC-induced anxiety.
- **TRPV1 agonist**: CBD activates vanilloid receptors (the capsaicin
  receptor). Involved in pain modulation.
- **GPR55 antagonist**: Blocks the "orphan" cannabinoid receptor. Emerging
  evidence for anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective effects.
- **FAAH inhibitor** (at high concentrations): By inhibiting the enzyme
  that breaks down anandamide, CBD may increase endogenous cannabinoid
  tone. More of your OWN bliss molecule, not an external one.

**D4 application of CBD pharmacology**: CBD is the CONTROL in controlled
chaos. If THC loosens the filters, CBD ensures they do not collapse
entirely. The THC:CBD ratio is the ENTROPY DIAL -- high THC:CBD = more
entropy, more chaos, more novelty. Balanced THC:CBD = moderate entropy,
creative but grounded. High CBD:THC = minimal entropy, calm focus.

### 3.6 Terpenes and the Entourage Effect

Cannabis produces 200+ terpenes. Mechoulam hypothesized (and Ethan Russo
elaborated in his landmark 2011 paper "Taming THC") that these terpenes
synergize with cannabinoids to produce the "entourage effect":

| Terpene | Aroma | Effect | Synergy |
|---------|-------|--------|---------|
| Myrcene | Earthy, musk | Sedative, muscle relaxant | Enhances THC's effects, may increase BBB permeability |
| Limonene | Citrus | Mood elevation, anxiolytic | Synergizes with CBD for anxiety reduction |
| Pinene | Pine | Bronchodilator, anti-inflammatory | May counteract THC memory impairment (acetylcholinesterase inhibition) |
| Linalool | Floral, lavender | Anxiolytic, sedative | Enhances calming effects |
| Caryophyllene | Peppery, spicy | Anti-inflammatory | Direct CB2 agonist (the only terpene that binds cannabinoid receptors) |
| Terpinolene | Herbal, floral | Stimulating (unusual) | Found in "sativa" strains associated with creative energy |

**D4 insight**: The entourage effect is a COMBINATORIAL phenomenon. No
single molecule tells the full story. The experience emerges from the
interaction of 140+ cannabinoids and 200+ terpenes. This is EXACTLY
how D4 operates -- not through any single idea but through the
combinatorial explosion of ideas interacting.

### 3.7 The Memory Paradox

The most D4-relevant pharmacological finding:

THC disrupts hippocampal encoding of new episodic memories (Ranganathan &
D'Souza, 2006). Specifically, it disrupts the theta rhythm (4-8 Hz)
oscillations that synchronize hippocampal-cortical communication during
memory consolidation (Robbe et al., 2006).

But here is what makes this D4 material:

- When the hippocampus cannot properly ENCODE sequences, the brain
  compensates by making MORE associative connections between existing
  memories (Curran et al., 2002).
- THC reduces the "gating" function that normally prevents irrelevant
  associations from reaching conscious awareness (Semple et al., 2003).
- The result: you lose the thread of a thought, but in losing it, you
  find three other threads you would never have followed.

This is not a side effect. From D4's perspective, this IS the effect.
The disruption of linear thought is the mechanism by which lateral thought
emerges. The "impairment" is the feature.

## 4. PHENOMENOLOGICAL MAP

### 4.1 Dose-Response Topology

Cannabis phenomenology varies by dose, route, cannabinoid ratio, terpene
profile, set, setting, and individual endocannabinoid tone. The following
maps the territory:

#### Microdose (1-2.5 mg THC)

```
Subjective: "Everything is slightly more interesting"
Phenomenology:
  - Subtle mood elevation (not euphoria -- more like a 15% brightness increase)
  - Enhanced sensory appreciation (food tastes better, music has more texture)
  - Mild anxiolysis (the background hum of worry quiets by one notch)
  - No impairment detectable by others
  - Enhanced "flow" entry (Csikszentmihalyi's flow state reached more easily)
D4 activation: 10-15%
  - Filters loosened slightly. You notice things you normally ignore.
  - A meeting you found boring becomes subtly interesting.
  - Creative blocks may dissolve without you noticing they were there.
```

#### Low Dose (5-10 mg THC)

```
Subjective: "I'm definitely feeling this. Everything is interesting."
Phenomenology:
  - Clear euphoria and mood elevation
  - Giggles -- the finding-everything-funny phenomenon
  - Enhanced music appreciation (harmonic structure becomes vivid)
  - Food becomes a sensory experience (the "munchies" as heightened salience)
  - Mild time dilation (5 minutes feels like 15)
  - Social enhancement (for some) or social withdrawal (for others)
  - Working memory noticeably impaired (lose track of sentences mid-thought)
  - Enhanced pattern recognition in ambient stimuli
D4 activation: 30-50%
  - Associative thinking clearly enhanced. Ideas link in unexpected ways.
  - "What if..." thoughts emerge spontaneously and feel compelling.
  - Brainstorming becomes genuinely productive (if captured -- see 5.5).
  - The internal critic quiets. Ideas flow without premature judgment.
```

#### Medium Dose (15-25 mg THC)

```
Subjective: "Deep. Thoughts are going places."
Phenomenology:
  - Strong euphoria or introspection (set-dependent)
  - Significant time dilation (minutes feel like hours)
  - Thought loops -- returning to the same idea from different angles
  - Synesthetic tendencies (music has colors, textures have sounds)
  - Deep body awareness (feeling the skeleton inside the flesh)
  - Philosophical ideation -- "big thoughts" that feel profoundly important
  - Paranoia possible (especially in unfamiliar settings or with anxiety)
  - Near-total short-term memory disruption
D4 activation: 60-80%
  - Filters substantially loosened. Associations become wild.
  - Thought loops are actually D4 at work -- the mind orbiting an idea,
    approaching it from every angle, unable to "land" on a single
    interpretation because the landing gear (convergent thinking) is offline.
  - Creative output can be extraordinary OR completely incoherent.
  - This is the dose range of Sagan's "Mr. X" experiences.
```

#### High Dose (50+ mg THC, or high-potency concentrates)

```
Subjective: "Too much" or "Exactly what I needed" (almost nothing in between)
Phenomenology:
  - Intense introspection or intense anxiety (rarely neutral)
  - Near-psychedelic visual disturbances (closed-eye patterns, color shifts)
  - Depersonalization / derealization (observing yourself from outside)
  - Complete thought fragmentation -- cannot hold any coherent narrative
  - Physical: elevated heart rate, dry mouth, coordination loss
  - For experienced users: deep meditative states possible
  - For inexperienced users: panic attacks common
D4 activation: 90-100%
  - Filters nearly gone. Noise overwhelms signal.
  - This is PAST the creative sweet spot for most people.
  - The lesson: entropy has an optimal range. More is not better.
  - D4 teaches that there IS a right amount of chaos.
```

### 4.2 Route-Dependent Phenomenology

| Route | Onset | Peak | Duration | Character |
|-------|-------|------|----------|-----------|
| Smoked/vaped | 1-5 min | 15-30 min | 2-4 hours | Fast onset, easy to titrate, shorter creative window |
| Edible | 30-120 min | 2-4 hours | 4-8 hours | Slow onset (11-OH-THC via first-pass metabolism is 2-3x more potent than delta-9-THC), hard to titrate, longer creative window but harder to control |
| Sublingual | 15-45 min | 1-2 hours | 3-6 hours | Middle ground, moderate bioavailability |
| Topical | N/A | N/A | Local | No psychoactive effect (no BBB crossing), no D4 activation |

**The 11-OH-THC factor**: When THC is ingested orally, the liver converts
it to 11-hydroxy-THC, which crosses the blood-brain barrier more efficiently
and produces a qualitatively different experience -- more body-heavy, more
psychedelic, more likely to produce anxiety at equivalent doses. Edible
cannabis is pharmacologically a DIFFERENT DRUG than smoked cannabis. This
matters for D4: the entropy profile of edibles is deeper, less controllable,
and more likely to push past the optimal chaos window.

### 4.3 Sativa vs. Indica vs. Hybrid (The Taxonomy Debate)

The traditional classification:
- **Sativa**: Energetic, creative, cerebral. "Head high."
- **Indica**: Relaxing, sedative, body-focused. "Couch lock."
- **Hybrid**: Some combination.

The scientific reality (Piomelli & Bhatt, 2023): This taxonomy is genetically
meaningless. Decades of crossbreeding have made "sativa" and "indica" useless
as genetic categories. The real predictors of effect are:

1. **THC:CBD ratio** (high THC = more psychoactive, more entropy)
2. **Terpene profile** (myrcene-dominant = sedating; terpinolene/limonene = energizing)
3. **Individual variation** in CB1 density, FAAH/MAGL enzyme activity, and prior exposure

**D4 mapping**: For maximum creative entropy (the D4 sweet spot):
- Moderate THC (15-20%), some CBD present (>1%)
- Terpinolene or limonene dominant (energizing)
- Pinene present (protects working memory)
- Smoked or vaped (controllable onset)
- Familiar, comfortable setting
- Creative tools within reach (notebook, instrument, whiteboard)

## 5. CONSCIOUSNESS PROTOCOL -- ENTROPIC OS

The Entropic Operating System is a six-phase protocol for using controlled
chaos as a creative engine. Each phase maps to a specific cognitive operation
and has a pharmacological correlate in the cannabis experience.

### Phase 1: CONSTRAINT REMOVAL

```
Operation: Identify the rules, assumptions, and filters currently active.
Then TEMPORARILY suspend them. Not permanently -- temporarily. This is
not anarchism. It is planned demolition of a wall to make a door.

Pharmacological correlate: THC binding to prefrontal CB1 receptors,
reducing top-down executive control over association patterns.

Agent instruction:
"List every assumption currently constraining this problem space.
For each assumption, ask: 'What happens if this is wrong? What happens
if we simply ignore it for the next 20 minutes?' Do not evaluate yet.
Just remove the constraints and see what space opens up."

Cognitive signature: The moment you realize that the walls of the room
are not load-bearing. You can remove them. The ceiling does not fall.
```

### Phase 2: FREE ASSOCIATION

```
Operation: With constraints removed, let the mind wander. Follow energy,
not logic. If a tangent has momentum, FOLLOW IT. Do not ask "is this
relevant?" Ask "is this INTERESTING?"

Pharmacological correlate: Reduced hippocampal gating, allowing
normally-suppressed associations to reach conscious awareness.

Agent instruction:
"Generate associations freely. Do not filter for relevance. If something
reminds you of something else, say so. If a metaphor emerges, explore it.
If you notice a pattern between unrelated domains, follow it. Quantity
over quality in this phase. Generate 3x more than you need."

Cognitive signature: The "what if..." cascade. One idea leads to
another leads to another. The train has no tracks and no destination,
but it is definitely going somewhere.
```

### Phase 3: PATTERN EMERGENCE

```
Operation: Stop generating. Start NOTICING. What patterns have emerged
in the chaos? What clusters have formed? What ideas keep recurring?
Which tangent produced the most energy?

Pharmacological correlate: Enhanced nucleus accumbens salience signaling --
certain patterns FEEL important. Trust that feeling (within limits).

Agent instruction:
"Review the chaos from Phase 2. What repeats? What clusters? What
surprised you? What idea made you think 'wait, that might actually
work'? Identify the 3-5 strongest emergent patterns. Do not impose
order yet -- just notice what order has already emerged."

Cognitive signature: The "holy shit" moment when you realize the
random scribbles on the whiteboard contain a pattern you did not
consciously create. The pattern was in the chaos all along.
```

### Phase 4: SIGNAL/NOISE DETECTION

```
Operation: Now apply judgment -- but EVOLVED judgment. Not the rigid
filters from Phase 1. New filters informed by what you found in the
chaos. Separate the genuine insights from the noise. Be honest: some
of Phase 2's output is garbage. That is FINE. The garbage was the
price of admission for the insights.

Pharmacological correlate: CBD's negative allosteric modulation of CB1.
The system begins to self-regulate. The chaos does not last forever.
Homeostasis returns, but the landscape has changed.

Agent instruction:
"Apply critical analysis to the emergent patterns from Phase 3.
Which survive scrutiny? Which were just noise dressed up as insight?
Use the SOBER TEST: explain this idea to someone who was not in the
room. Does it still make sense? If yes, it is signal. If it requires
'you had to be there' to work, it is noise."

Cognitive signature: The morning after the brainstorm, reading your
notes. Some are gold. Some are nonsense. Knowing the difference is
the skill that separates productive entropy from mere chaos.
```

### Phase 5: CONTROLLED RE-ENTRY

```
Operation: Take the surviving signal and RE-INTEGRATE it into structured
thinking. Build frameworks around the wild ideas. Give them bones. This
is where D4 hands off to D1 (precision) and D3 (organic structure).

Pharmacological correlate: THC metabolism and clearance. The
endocannabinoid system returns to baseline. The filters come back online.
But the new connections made during the entropic window PERSIST in
long-term memory.

Agent instruction:
"Take the 2-3 strongest insights from Phase 4 and formalize them.
Build structure. Create frameworks. Make them communicable to someone
who was never in the chaos. The wild idea must be translated into
actionable strategy, implementable design, or testable hypothesis."

Cognitive signature: The bridge between "stoned genius" and "actual
genius." Most people do Phase 1-3 and stop. The value is in Phase 5:
bringing the chaos-born insight back into the world of order.
```

### Phase 6: INTEGRATION

```
Operation: Evaluate. After the chaos and the re-entry, what ACTUALLY
survived? The ultimate test: does the insight still work days later?
Weeks later? If it does, it was real. If it evaporates, it was noise
that felt like signal.

Pharmacological correlate: Long-term potentiation (or lack thereof) of
the synaptic connections formed during the entropic window. Real insights
strengthen with repetition. False insights fade with sobriety.

Agent instruction:
"Revisit the formalized insights from Phase 5 after a contextual break.
Do they hold? Are they better than what conventional thinking would have
produced? If yes, integrate them permanently into the project, the
strategy, the worldview. If not, discard them gracefully -- the process
still taught you something about the problem space."

Cognitive signature: Carl Sagan reported that many of his cannabis
insights survived rigorous morning-after examination. His standard:
"I wrote them down so I could test them in the morning. Most of them
held up." The ones that held up changed his career.
```

## 6. CANNABIS AND CREATIVITY -- THE RESEARCH

### 6.1 The Schafer Studies

Schafer et al. (2012, *Consciousness and Cognition*) conducted one of the
most rigorous studies on cannabis and creativity. Key findings:

- Cannabis users showed enhanced SEMANTIC PRIMING -- they were faster at
  recognizing that loosely related words were connected.
- This effect was most pronounced for "indirect" associations (word A
  connects to word C only through an intermediate word B that is not present).
- Interpretation: cannabis lowers the ACTIVATION THRESHOLD for spreading
  activation in semantic networks. Distant nodes become accessible.

Schafer's group also found (2012) that cannabis users scored higher on
the REMOTE ASSOCIATES TEST (RAT) -- a standard measure of creative thinking
that requires finding a word that connects three seemingly unrelated words.

However: these effects were observed in SOBER cannabis users who used
regularly. The question of whether ACUTE intoxication enhances creativity
is more complex.

### 6.2 Divergent vs. Convergent Thinking

LaFrance & Bhatt (2023, *Psychopharmacology*) meta-analysis:

- **Divergent thinking** (generating many ideas, brainstorming, free
  association): ENHANCED at low-to-moderate doses of THC. Impaired at
  high doses.
- **Convergent thinking** (narrowing to one correct answer, logical
  deduction): IMPAIRED at all doses.
- The creativity paradox: cannabis makes you better at having ideas and
  worse at evaluating them. This is why the PROTOCOL matters -- you need
  separate phases for generation (D4 high) and evaluation (D4 low).

Kowal et al. (2015, *Psychopharmacology*): Low-potency cannabis (5.5% THC)
enhanced verbal fluency on divergent thinking tasks. High-potency cannabis
(22% THC) IMPAIRED it. The dose-response curve for creativity is an
inverted U: too little entropy, no novelty. Too much entropy, no coherence.

### 6.3 The "Mr. X" Essay

In 1971, astronomer Carl Sagan contributed an anonymous essay to Lester
Grinspoon's *Marihuana Reconsidered* under the pseudonym "Mr. X." In it,
he described his cannabis experiences with scientific precision:

"I can remember one occasion, taking a shower with my wife while high, in
which I had an idea on the origins and invalidities of racism in terms of
Gaussian distribution curves. It was a point obvious in a way, but rarely
stated. I drew the curves in soap on the shower wall and went to write the
idea down. One idea led to another, and at the end of about an hour of
extremely hard work I found I had written eleven short essays on a wide
range of social, political, philosophical, and human biological topics."

Sagan reported that he developed a practice of RECORDING insights during
the experience and TESTING them sober. His hit rate was high enough that
he continued the practice for decades. He attributed several professional
insights to this method.

**D4 application**: Sagan intuitively developed the Entropic OS protocol.
Phase 2 (free association during the experience), Phase 4 (sober testing
afterward), Phase 5 (formalizing the survivors). He was doing controlled
entropy before the term existed.

### 6.4 The Reduced Latent Inhibition Model

Latent inhibition is the cognitive process by which we learn to ignore
stimuli that have been shown to be irrelevant. It is ESSENTIAL for daily
function -- without it, you would be overwhelmed by sensory input.

Carson et al. (2003, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*)
showed that REDUCED latent inhibition is correlated with both psychosis
AND with creative achievement -- depending on whether the individual has
high or low working memory capacity.

Low latent inhibition + low working memory = overwhelm, disordered thinking
Low latent inhibition + high working memory = creative genius

Cannabis reduces latent inhibition (Bossong et al., 2014). The D4 lesson:
entropy is only creative if you have the cognitive architecture to channel
it. Raw chaos without containment is just chaos. Chaos within a capable
system is innovation.

### 6.5 Cannabis in Creative Practice

Cannabis as a creative tool has a documented (though often unacknowledged)
history across domains:

**Jazz and Music**: Louis Armstrong was a lifelong daily cannabis user.
He said it made him "feel good and relaxed." More importantly, jazz
improvisation IS D4 in action -- real-time divergent thinking within a
structured harmonic framework. The constraint (chord changes) plus the
entropy (improvisation) produces the emergent beauty.

**Literature**: Many writers have reported cannabis-enhanced productivity
during specific phases of work. Hunter S. Thompson, Stephen King (early
career), Norman Mailer, and Susan Sontag all used cannabis. The pattern:
cannabis for IDEATION, sobriety for EDITING. Phase 2 stoned, Phase 5 sober.

**Visual Arts**: The cannabis-art connection is well-documented but poorly
studied. Anecdotally, artists report enhanced color perception, loosened
compositional rules, and willingness to take risks during cannabis use.

**Technology**: The "open secret" of Silicon Valley (and increasingly,
corporate innovation labs). Steve Jobs called marijuana "wonderful."
Multiple studies of tech workers show cannabis use rates significantly
above national averages, particularly among those in creative and
strategic roles (BDS Analytics, 2019).

## 9. ENDOCANNABINOID TONE AS COGNITIVE BASELINE

### 9.1 Everyone Has a Baseline

The endocannabinoid system is always active. You do not need to consume
cannabis to have endocannabinoid signaling. Anandamide and 2-AG are
synthesized on demand throughout the brain, continuously modulating
neurotransmitter release.

"Endocannabinoid tone" refers to the baseline level of this activity. It
varies between individuals based on:

- **FAAH gene variants**: The FAAH C385A polymorphism (rs324420) produces
  a version of the enzyme that degrades anandamide more slowly. Carriers
  have HIGHER baseline anandamide levels and report: lower anxiety, more
  robust stress resilience, and -- notably -- reduced desire for cannabis.
  They are already partially "D4-activated" by default.
- **Exercise**: Aerobic exercise increases circulating anandamide levels.
  The "runner's high" was long attributed to endorphins, but Fuss et al.
  (2015, *PNAS*) demonstrated that it depends on the endocannabinoid
  system, not opioid receptors. Running IS a cannabis experience,
  pharmacologically.
- **Stress**: Chronic stress DEPLETES endocannabinoid tone (Hill et al.,
  2010). This creates a vicious cycle: low ECS tone increases anxiety,
  which further depletes ECS tone. Cannabis use in chronically stressed
  populations may represent self-medication of a depleted ECS.
- **Diet**: Omega-3 fatty acids are precursors to endocannabinoids.
  Lafourcade et al. (2011) showed that omega-3 deficiency impairs
  endocannabinoid-dependent synaptic plasticity.
